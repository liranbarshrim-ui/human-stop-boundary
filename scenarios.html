Where accountability breaks

These are normal operations. What's missing is not data, policy, or review. It's a named authority to stop. When there is no named stop authority, even good people do not stop.

Clinical Governance

A hospital protocol is updated. The change affects drug dosage for a specific patient population.

The update goes live automatically after approval by committee.

Three people reviewed it. No one was named to stop it.

Two weeks later, adverse events are reported.

The damage Patient harm. Regulatory investigation. No individual accountability. The committee approved. No one stopped.

Who was required to stop this before it became irreversible?

No name exists.

Financial Services

An algorithmic trading system flags unusual market conditions. The model continues executing.

The parameters were set six months ago.

The risk committee meets weekly. The next meeting is in four days.

By then, the position is irreversible.

The damage Significant financial exposure. Post-mortem review. Personal liability for the risk officer — who was never named to stop.

Who had the authority to stop execution before that point?

No single name. No pre-committed stop.

Agentic AI

A deployment pipeline pushes an updated model to production.

The model interacts with 40,000 users before anyone reviews outputs.

The team approved the release. No individual was named to halt it if behavior deviated.

Behavior deviated. No one stopped it.

The damage Reputational and regulatory exposure at scale. Internal review finds no one with explicit stop authority. Everyone was responsible. No one was named.

Who was required to stop this before it reached scale?

The answer was "the team." The team is not a name.
