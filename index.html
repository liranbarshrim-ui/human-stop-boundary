The Architecture of Accountability

Systems move. Decisions execute. Outcomes become irreversible.
The question is not what happened.
It's who was named to stop it before it became irreversible.

Exposure If an irreversible decision causes harm, responsibility will not remain abstract.

It will resolve to a name.

If no name was defined in advance, accountability will be assigned after the fact.

This question is already emerging in regulatory and board-level contexts.

"We" removes the need for a single person to stop.

When a decision becomes irreversible under uncertainty, someone must be named to stop it.

The Problem

No One Stops

"We" is where responsibility disappears.

"We" diffuses responsibility. It creates a gap where oversight exists in language but not in reality.

The Imperative

One Name

Critical outcomes must map to a single name. Not a function. Not a team. A person.

The Law

Responsibility Before Failure

Responsibility must exist before failure, not after.

The Threshold

Irreversibility

When uncertainty meets irreversible impact, delay becomes decision.

The Authority

Stop Authority

Authority to stop exists only if it is real, supported, and usable.

The Erosion

How Responsibility Disappears

Responsibility erodes gradually inside systems. The named person is a fragile achievement.
