Named human stop authority in autonomous systems, defined before irreversible execution occurs.

DAR Framework Human Stop Authority AI Governance MiCA / CASP Autonomous Systems
Principle

The authority to initiate is not the authority to terminate.

Autonomous systems may execute decisions. Responsibility remains human.

The gap between information and named stop authority is not a structural bug. It is an intentional feature designed to avoid personal accountability.

There is a moment when you can stop.
And there is a moment when all you can do
is explain why no one did.

Formal Definition

Decision Accountability Record (DAR) assigns a named human authority responsible for stopping automated systems before irreversible execution occurs.

Not a committee. Not a policy. A name.

Purpose

DAR ensures that automated systems operating in critical environments cannot execute irreversible actions without an identified human authority responsible for halting execution.

If authority needs to be reconstructed after an incident, it was not defined before it. Compliance confirms that rules were followed. DAR confirms that someone was authorized to stop before the rules ran out.

Reference Case

The 2012 Knight Capital collapse demonstrated how automated systems can execute catastrophic, irreversible actions when no single individual holds clear stop authority. $440 million in losses in 45 minutes. The system ran. No one stopped it.

The question regulation rarely asks

Who holds the authority to stop the system
when execution becomes irreversible?

By name.
Positioning

The principle of named stop authority already exists in critical systems. It is beginning to appear in academic discussions of AI governance. DAR frames it as a simple question regulation rarely asks: who, by name, can stop the system before execution becomes irreversible?

Systems that cannot stop are not autonomous. They are ungoverned.
