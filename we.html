The Death of "We"

Liran Bar-Shrim
I

The Death of "We"

In the boardroom, everyone says "We."

"We approved."

"We're moving forward."

It sounds safe. It feels aligned.

It isn't.

When something goes wrong, "We" disappears. No one is responsible, because everyone was involved.

Responsibility shared by everyone
is responsibility avoided by anyone.

It behaves like gas.
It expands to fill the space.
It applies no pressure.
It supports no weight.

This is not a communication problem. It is a structural failure.

II

The Illusion of Safety

Consensus is mistaken for control.

If enough people agree, it must be correct.

If enough people approve, it must be safe.

But consensus does something else: it distributes discomfort.

No single person carries the weight of stopping. No single person feels the decision fully.

And when no one feels it, no one stops it.
III

The Missing Function

Every system has functions:

Initiate Review Approve Execute Stop — missing

Not as a possibility. As a requirement.

When stopping is optional, progress becomes automatic. Automatic progress, under uncertainty, is how systems fail.

A system cannot hesitate. It cannot feel consequence. It can only continue.

Without someone explicitly required to stop, there is no real stopping point. There is only motion.

And motion does not stop itself.
IV

What Actually Fails

Failures rarely begin with intent. They begin with motion.

A process starts.

Momentum builds.

Signals appear.

No one is required to interrupt.

Everyone assumes someone else will. And so it continues.

Good people cross lines. Not because they intend to. Because nothing requires them to stop.

Systems reward progress, not hesitation. Without an explicit requirement, continuation is the default.

And for a while, it works.

The system moves. Decisions are made. Nothing appears broken.

Until it doesn't.

V

The Line No One Sees

Most systems do not fail at the moment of decision. They fail at the moment the decision becomes irreversible.

That moment is rarely marked. It is an undefined moment.

→   "We can still stop"
↓
—   No alert. No meeting. No escalation.
↓
→   "We are already committed."

This is not a cliff. It is a slope.

And once that line is crossed, approval no longer matters. Only stopping does.

When no one is required to stop, the risk does not disappear.

It concentrates. On whoever signs.
VI

What Regulation Doesn't Define

Regulation defines roles. Requires oversight. Enforces reporting.

But it focuses on what happens before and after a decision. It rarely defines the moment itself:

Who is explicitly required to stop the process
when it becomes irreversible under uncertainty?

Responsibility exists.

Documentation exists.

The authority to stop is undefined.

That is where organizations break.

VII

The Structural Shift

The solution is not more oversight. Not more consensus. Not more layers.

It is one change: replace "We" with a name.

Before anything becomes irreversible, one person is explicitly required to say: Stop.

Not responsible.

Required.

When everyone owns the decision, no one actually holds it. And if no one holds it, it does not stop.

Naming a person creates tension.
It removes ambiguity.
It makes decisions heavier.

That is exactly why it is avoided.
VIII

What Changes

The moment a single name is defined in advance, everything shifts.

Decisions slow down.

Questions sharpen.

Uncertainty becomes real.

Same people. Same process.

But now, someone is required to stop.

And that changes everything.

IX

The Principle

Accountability does not begin after failure.

It begins at the moment someone is required to stop the process.

If stopping is optional, failure is structural.

If stopping is required, decisions become real.

X

The End of "We"

"We" is comfortable.

"We" is collaborative.

"We" cannot stop.

Only someone can.

In your organization, when a decision becomes irreversible under uncertainty, is there a name?

Not a role. Not a committee.

A name.

If not, the system will continue.

Until it doesn't.

And by then, it is no longer a decision.
