The Death of "We"

I

In the boardroom, everyone says "We."

"We approved."

"We're moving forward."

It sounds safe. It feels aligned.

It isn't.

When something goes wrong, "We" disappears. No one is responsible.

Responsibility shared by everyone
is responsibility avoided by anyone.

It behaves like gas.
It expands to fill the space.
It applies no pressure.
It supports no weight.

II

Consensus is mistaken for control.

If enough people agree, it must be correct.

If enough people approve, it must be safe.

No one feels the weight of stopping.

And when no one feels it, no one stops it.

III

Every system has functions:

Initiate Review Approve Execute Stop — missing

Without stopping, progress becomes automatic.

Without someone explicitly required to stop, there is only motion.

IV

A process starts.

Momentum builds.

No one interrupts.

And so it continues.

V

"We can still stop"
↓
No alert. No escalation.
↓
"We are already committed"
This is not a cliff. It is a slope.

VI

Replace "We" with a name.

One person required to stop.

That is exactly why it is avoided.
Who is named to stop an irreversible decision?
After every failure, the same line appears:

"Someone should have stopped this."

The only question:
Who was supposed to stop it — and why was that never defined in advance.
DAR Name the stop.
